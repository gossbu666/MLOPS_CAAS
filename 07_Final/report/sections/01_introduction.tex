\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:introduction}

\section{Problem Statement}

Chiang Mai, Thailand, experiences severe seasonal air pollution events driven by
agricultural burning and forest fires between February and April each year.
PM2.5 concentrations routinely exceed 150~\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} during
haze season --- more than six times the WHO guideline of 15~\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3}
and three times Thailand's national standard of 50~\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3}.
The health impact is substantial: elevated PM2.5 is associated with respiratory
disease, cardiovascular events, and premature mortality, placing particular burden
on children, the elderly, and outdoor workers.

Despite the severity of the problem, no publicly accessible automated forecasting
service provides Chiang Mai residents with reliable multi-day PM2.5 predictions.
Current government monitoring (air4thai, PCD Thailand) reports measured values
but does not forecast future concentrations or issue advance hazard warnings,
leaving citizens unable to plan protective behaviour.

\section{Objectives}

This project --- the \textbf{ChiangMai Air Quality Alert System (CAAS)} ---
addresses this gap by building a complete, end-to-end MLOps pipeline that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Forecasts PM2.5} at three horizons: next day (t+1), three days ahead (t+3),
          and seven days ahead (t+7), using 15 years of historical data (2011--2025).
    \item \textbf{Issues binary hazard alerts} when PM2.5 is forecast to exceed
          50~\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3}, enabling early protective action.
    \item \textbf{Integrates NASA FIRMS fire hotspot data} alongside meteorological
          variables to capture the dominant driver of seasonal PM2.5 spikes.
    \item \textbf{Deploys as a production-grade MLOps pipeline} with automated
          data ingestion, drift monitoring, champion/challenger model governance,
          and CI/CD-triggered retraining.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions}

The key contributions of this project are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A 45-feature dataset spanning 5,257 days (2011--2025), merging three
          heterogeneous sources: PCD PM2.5 measurements, Open-Meteo weather
          reanalysis, and NASA FIRMS fire hotspot counts.
    \item An XGBoost champion model achieving MAE of 5.31, 6.91, and
          8.34~\textmu g/m\textsuperscript{3} at t+1, t+3, and t+7 respectively,
          with AUROC~$\geq$~0.96 across all horizons.
    \item A SHAP-based interpretability analysis demonstrating that the dominant
          predictive signal shifts from recent PM2.5 lags (short horizons) to
          fire hotspot accumulation and seasonal position (longer horizons).
    \item An ablation study quantifying the contribution of FIRMS fire data:
          removing six fire-derived features degrades MAE by 4.3--6.8\% across horizons.
    \item A complete MLOps stack: FastAPI inference service, Streamlit monitoring
          dashboard, Evidently drift monitoring with automated GitHub Actions
          retraining triggers, and Terraform-provisioned AWS infrastructure.
\end{itemize}
